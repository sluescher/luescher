# An Ink-Stained Life: The Marginalia of a Sixteenth-Century Cairene Scholar
&nbsp;

![A picture of two folios of al-Muẓaffarī's personal register of notes, also called "thabat"'](/images/thabat.png)
*A picture of two folios of al-Muẓaffarī's personal register of notes, also called "thabat" (Rabat, MS Unknown, fols. 112v-113r)*

&nbsp;

This dissertation reconstructs the life trajectory, documentary practices, and book consumption of Muḥammad al-Muẓaffarī (d. ca. 950/1543), a minor scholar active in late Mamluk and early Ottoman Cairo, through 665 marginal annotations he wrote in manuscripts now dispersed across libraries worldwide. Al-Muẓaffarī’s notes and his sizeable personal book collection demonstrate that non-elite scholars had substantial access to the intellectual world of late Mamluk Cairo and actively participated in its scholarly community. They sat in mosques, madrasas, shops, and private homes, hearing ḥadīth, copying texts, and writing annotations, generating substantial quantities of documentary material—certificates, reading records, and other marginalia.

Mamluk readers treated books as living archives, documenting their interactions with them, giving us a rare glimpse into the reading practices of a premodern society. Biographical dictionaries draw heavily on these materials, but generally do not acknowledge them, obscuring the contributions of non-elite scholars. When modern scholarship relies primarily on these curated sources, it inevitably reproduces their silences and distortions. Reconstructing a more complete picture of the scholarly community requires a methodological shift toward alternative sources, such as manuscript marginalia, and studying the figures at its margins to reveal its full breadth, as well as the practices and norms that structured it.

Al-Muẓaffarī exemplifies the invisible scholars whose labor sustained the scholarly community but remained largely absent from biographical dictionaries and chronicles. Over five decades, he meticulously documented his intellectual pursuits—which texts he read, with whom, and when—leaving a paper trail that traces his engagement with the scholarly community. The sustained dedication, however, never translated into scholarly recognition or advancement. His trajectory exposes the tension between scholarly ideals and social realities in late Mamluk Cairo, where structural advantages—family lineage, kinship networks, educational ties, political patronage, and economic resources—significantly influenced scholars’ success more than individual merit, relegating al-Muẓaffarī to the margins. By centering him and his notes, this dissertation reveals how the scholarly community functioned beneath the polished narratives of traditional sources.
